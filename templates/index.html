Approximating π :)

Let's calculate pi! The number pi is irrational, meaning that it has an infinite number of digits without any discernible pattern. Pi is also a special number, representing the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. It appears in many formulae across mathematics and physics.

The first approximations of pi can be traced back to Ancient Greece. Archimedes, a Greek mathematician, devised a method to approximate pi using polygons. Let's first take a look on how he did it!

Archimedes' Method

We know that we can calculate pi by dividing the circumference of a circle by its diameter. But how do we calculate a circle's circumference without pi? We can't, so let's do our best by approximating it with polygons.

To start, let's inscribe a regular hexagon inside the circle, then circumscribe a regular hexagon outside the circle. We know that the perimeter of the inscribed hexagon is smaller than that of the circle while the perimeter of the circumscribed hexagon is larger than that of the circle. By calculating these perimeters and dividing them by the diameter of the circle, we get two values approximating pi: one slightly less and one slightly greater.
